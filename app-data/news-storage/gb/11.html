'Perfect for Pep': Cole Palmer is earning rave reviews at Man City after breakthrough year

Manchester City youngster Cole Palmer has enjoyed a breakthrough year at the club to the delight of those who know him well

Talk to anyone who has coached Cole Palmer and it won't be long in the conversation before they bring up his brain.

The Manchester City youngster has plenty going for him, with a crisp touch, the ability to glide past defenders keeping the ball at his feet, and an eye for goal: as one senior club official puts it though, 'Cole's brain is the best thing about him'.

It's coming up to a year since the 19-year-old began training regularly with Pep Guardiola's first team on a regular basis and what a year it has been: the highs have included a full debut, Champions League appearance and scoring the winner in the FA Youth Cup final; on the other side of that has been the debilitating effect of Covid and the shocking tragedy of friend and former teammate Jeremy Wisten dying that forced many in the football world to pause.

At the end of such an eventful year, Palmer's star has never been brighter. His 13 goals in City's successful Premier League 2 campaign were second in the league only to teammate Liam Delap's monstrous 24 strikes, and of the emerging talent at the club Palmer's ability to play multiple positions looks particularly promising given the versatility Guardiola demands.

On Friday night, he was on a star-studded substitutes bench at Newcastle alongside the likes of Phil Foden and Riyad Mahrez.

Such progress comes as no surprise to those who have followed his career from the very beginning.

Graeme Fowler was a coach at local club NJ Wythenshawe when he spotted a four-year-old running rings around older kids at a trial for the Under-7s.

"I walked in the field and saw this little blonde kid who was only dead small and just running with the ball. At that age, the kids just chase the ball and he had 10 kids chasing him. He was running with it and would just do a simple turn and go the other way and the kids would then follow him the other way. They couldn't get the ball off him, wherever he turned, and he was a year younger than them all.

"He was miles above everyone brain-wise. His technical ability was really good but I had a lot of good technical players but his brain was just so far advanced for a footballing brain at a young age it was incredible.

"He was doing stuff at seven and eight that I didn't see 14-year-olds do. His quickness of thought on the pitch, the things that he saw, and his technical ability to do stuff and be inventive, he was really good to watch. I always say that I had some good players but he stands out as the best player I've ever coached."

So advanced was Palmer's thinking at an early age that he would even infuriate his coaches in games, who would get ready in matches to tell him the decisions he should have made before realising that he'd seen a better one.

That ingenuity also saw United and City come calling very quickly despite the Wythenshawe lad not being blessed with the natural pace that usually drew the attention of the big clubs. City sold themselves best to the family and the move was finalised a few years later when he officially joined their Under-9s.

The player hasn't looked back but he has returned to watch his junior club whenever he could and remains in touch with them to this day; the local pub was as busy as the restrictions allowed when word reached of his imminent debut this season.

That connection has been part of the wider shaping that family and friends have had on Palmer's upbringing. His dad spent many long days and nights out practising on the local park with a football when he was young and his mum was also busy ferrying him to wherever he needed to be.

As well as keeping him grounded, they provided support when he was almost cut from the City academy in his teens as he was found lacking in his physical development; instead of spitting his dummy out, he went away and put in the hours to build his strength up and convince the club that they should keep him on.

Last year he bought his mum a new car as a small token of appreciation for everything, while it said a lot about his character and how he values friendships too that he chose two of the highlights of his burgeoning career to Jeremy Wisten.

Having dedicated his Champions League debut to him in post-match interviews, his first thought after scoring the winner in the FA Youth Cup final was to unveil a T-shirt to the camera bearing his friend's face alongside the message: "RIP Jeremy Wisten. 2002-2020. GBNF."

A thoughtful young man at home, it is what goes through his head on a football pitch that has seen him make great strides at City in the last year.

After taking confidence from his central role as Under-18 captain last season that included a thumping victory in the final of the Under-18 Premier League cup, the geographical restrictions placed on players in the pandemic allowed the youngster to join first-team training under Project Restart as the club prioritised those closest to home to help the senior squad prepare for matches.

Football is all about taking opportunities and Palmer certainly made the most of his, impressing Guardiola enough that he was named on the bench in the Premier League before the season was out. With Kyle Walker and Raheem Sterling helping to welcome him into the group, he quickly felt and looked at home among the star-studded squad.

"If you look at the way he plays, the first team I would highlight from Cole is his intelligence," one City insider said.

"He is a player who makes good decisions and I think when Pep sees someone that makes good decisions, all of a sudden he listens to the player. Plus all the talent and quality that you can tell from seeing Cole playing, he's got so many things that if he keeps pushing himself and keeps trying he's going to have an unbelievable career.

"He's a mix of talent but on top of that is intelligence and good decisions, which is key if you want to be successful so young in the first team."

A senior debut came as soon as September last year, alongside Kevin De Bruyne as a No.8 at Burnley in the Carabao Cup with Guardiola demanding a reaction from the shambolic 5-2 defeat to Leicester.

Having left his studs in on a tackle in the opening minutes, Palmer took a while to settle but grew into the game as it progressed and was unfortunate not to find the net. His manager beamed when asked after the game about the debutant and compared the finishing skills of this "incredible young lad" to Phil Foden.

A cameo at Marseille in the Champions League group stage has been his only other taste of first team football this season, yet the teenager has shown himself to be too good for Under-23 football.

The highlights reel from a season containing 13 goals and six assists from 16 appearances is as lengthy as it is enjoyable, and despite the quality of a thunderbolt against United or a wonderful no-look backheeled assist at home to Liverpool the best moment has to be the goal away to Liverpool in a stunning 7-2 win back in November.

Palmer had already bagged the opening goal by dispossessing a defender in the six-yard box and the young Blues were 2-0 up on the half-hour mark when Tommy Doyle raced onto a through-ball but saw his shot parried by the goalkeeper. There were seven Liverpool players in the box as the ball rolled to Palmer on the right edge of the box and all they could do was watch as with one magical, mesmeric touch he deftly chipped the ball over them all and into the far corner of the net.

The biggest challenge for the player now is to not get lost in the push for first-team football. City have had many talented youngsters that haven't made it in recent years and Palmer's versatility in being able to play centrally, wide or more forward could yet count against him. As he helps the first team to prepare for the Champions League final, whether to keep him close or send him out on loan is an important decision still to be made for next season.

For now though, he has never been more appreciated at City for the rare qualities that he brings and there will be many a pint sunk in Wythenshawe if he is able to continue on his path to the first team.

"He was dead small and because he was a year younger he looked even smaller but I always said he was going to make it and he is a perfect player for City's style of play now," said Fowler. "He's always calm on the ball. I know his hard work and his dedication. I've spoken to him all through growing up and I still speak to him now - he loves it and he is a hard worker. He's just passionate so I always knew he was going to make it.

"I always say to him he's a Pep player, he's a David Silva in the making. Everything is so flexible with positions but I see him in the David Silva role, behind the striker creating things and scoring. I couldn't be more proud.

"I've had a lot of kids who are good footballers but to make it that high up takes something special and he was from Day One. From day one his brain was just quicker than everyone else's and he had the technical ability to do it because he just practised every day."
